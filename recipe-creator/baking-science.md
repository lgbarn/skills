# Baking Science Reference

Scientific reference for why baking works the way it does. Load this file when
a user asks about baking, pastry, bread, cakes, cookies, pies, or any
flour-based preparation. Baking is chemistry — ratios and reactions matter
more here than in any other area of cooking.

---

## The Core Principle

Savory cooking is forgiving. Baking is not. The difference is that baking
relies on chemical and physical reactions that happen inside an oven where
you can't taste and adjust. Understanding the roles of each ingredient lets
you troubleshoot, substitute, and create with confidence.

---

## Flour: The Structural Backbone

### Protein Content Is Everything

Flour's protein (glutenin + gliadin) forms gluten when hydrated and worked.
More protein = more gluten potential = more structure and chew.

| Flour Type | Protein % | Best For | Character |
|-----------|-----------|----------|-----------|
| **Cake flour** | 7–8% | Tender cakes, delicate pastries | Soft, fine crumb, dissolves on the tongue |
| **Pastry flour** | 8–9% | Pie crust, biscuits, scones, cookies | Tender but with some body |
| **All-purpose (AP)** | 10–12% | General baking, cookies, quick breads | The compromise — works for most things |
| **Bread flour** | 12–14% | Yeast breads, pizza, bagels | Chewy, strong structure, good rise |
| **00 flour** | 11–12% | Pizza, fresh pasta | Finely milled, extensible rather than elastic |
| **Whole wheat** | 13–14% | Hearty breads, mixed into AP | High protein but bran cuts gluten strands |
| **Self-rising** | 8–9% | Biscuits, quick breads (Southern staple) | AP + baking powder + salt pre-mixed |

### Gluten: When to Develop vs. When to Avoid

**Develop gluten** (bread, pizza, bagels):
- Use bread flour (high protein)
- Add water, knead or stretch extensively
- Long fermentation develops gluten AND flavor
- The windowpane test: stretch dough thin — if it's translucent without
  tearing, gluten is developed

**Minimize gluten** (pie crust, biscuits, tender cakes):
- Use lower-protein flour (cake or pastry flour)
- Keep everything cold (cold inhibits gluten formation)
- Handle minimally — mix until JUST combined
- Fat coats flour particles, blocking water from reaching protein
- Acids (buttermilk, vinegar, lemon) tenderize by weakening gluten bonds
- Why Southern biscuits use self-rising flour + buttermilk + cold fat:
  low protein + acid + fat coating = tender, not tough

### Alternative Flours

| Flour | Properties | Substitution Notes |
|-------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Rice flour** | No gluten, gritty if used alone | Blend with starch for GF baking (70/30 rice flour/tapioca starch) |
| **Almond flour** | No gluten, high fat, moist | Cannot sub 1:1 for AP — needs binder (eggs, xanthan gum) |
| **Oat flour** | No gluten (if certified), absorbent | Good for cookies and pancakes, dense in bread |
| **Coconut flour** | Extremely absorbent, no gluten | Use 1/4 the amount of AP and add extra liquid/eggs |
| **Cornmeal** | No gluten, gritty texture | Cornbread, polenta — often mixed with AP for structure |
| **Teff flour** | No gluten, slightly sweet, earthy | Injera, blended into quick breads |
| **Cassava flour** | No gluten, closest to AP in behavior | Best 1:1 GF swap for AP in many recipes |

## Leavening: What Makes Things Rise

### Chemical Leaveners

**Baking soda (sodium bicarbonate):**
- Needs an acid to activate (buttermilk, yogurt, vinegar, lemon, brown sugar,
  honey, cocoa, cream of tartar)
- Reacts immediately on contact with acid — mix and bake promptly
- Also promotes Maillard browning (alkaline environment browns faster)
- Too much = metallic/soapy taste and yellow-green color
- Rule: 1/4 teaspoon per cup of flour is typical

**Baking powder:**
- Baking soda + acid + starch (built-in acid, no external acid needed)
- **Double-acting** (most common): first rise when wet, second rise in oven heat
- More forgiving than soda — batter can sit briefly before baking
- Rule: 1 to 1.5 teaspoons per cup of flour

**Why recipes use both:** Baking soda neutralizes excess acid in the batter
(preventing sour taste) AND provides browning. Baking powder provides the
primary lift. Together they balance flavor, color, and rise.

### Biological Leaveners

**Active dry yeast:**
- Must be proofed in warm water (105–110°F) with a pinch of sugar
- Dead if water is too hot (above 120°F kills it)
- Slower rise, more flavor development
- 1 packet = 2.25 teaspoons = enough for ~4 cups flour

**Instant (rapid-rise) yeast:**
- Mixed directly into dry ingredients, no proofing needed
- Finer granules, more cells per teaspoon
- Faster rise but slightly less complex flavor
- Use 25% less than active dry

**Sourdough starter:**
- Wild yeast + lactobacillus bacteria in a flour-water culture
- Produces CO2 (rise) + lactic and acetic acids (tang, flavor, preservation)
- Slower fermentation = more complex flavor development
- Active starter doubles in 4–8 hours after feeding
- The acidity strengthens gluten, creating chewy, open crumb

**How yeast works:** Yeast consumes sugars and produces CO2 gas + ethanol.
The CO2 inflates gluten networks (in bread) or batter structure (in enriched
doughs). The ethanol evaporates in the oven. Longer, slower fermentation
(cold retard in the fridge) produces more flavor compounds.

### Mechanical Leavening

- **Creaming** butter and sugar traps air bubbles in fat — those bubbles
  expand in oven heat (cake and cookie rise)
- **Whipping** eggs or egg whites creates foam structure — protein sets
  around air bubbles in heat (meringue, angel food, souffle)
- **Steam**: water converts to steam at 212°F, expanding ~1,600x in volume.
  This is what makes puff pastry, popovers, and choux rise. High initial
  oven heat is critical for steam leavening.
- **Lamination**: folding butter into dough creates hundreds of alternating
  layers. Steam from butter's water content pushes layers apart. This is
  croissants, puff pastry, and Danish.

## Fat: Tenderness, Flavor, and Structure

### How Fat Functions in Baking

| Function | Mechanism | Example |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| **Tenderizing** | Coats flour proteins, blocking gluten formation | Pie crust, cake |
| **Moisture** | Fat doesn't evaporate like water — keeps baked goods moist longer | Muffins, quick breads |
| **Flakiness** | Cold solid fat creates layers that melt into steam pockets | Biscuits, croissants, pie crust |
| **Leavening** | Creaming traps air; steam from butter's water content | Cakes, puff pastry |
| **Flavor** | Butter = diacetyl + lactones; lard = savory depth; oil = neutral | Everything |
| **Spread** | Melted fat spreads before structure sets | Cookie spread control |

### Fat Selection Guide

| Fat | Water Content | Best For | Character |
|-----|-------------|----------|-----------|
| **Butter (unsalted)** | ~18% water | Cakes, cookies, pastry, lamination | Rich flavor, flaky layers from water content |
| **Shortening (Crisco)** | 0% water | Pie crust, frosting, fried dough | Tender, no flavor — pure fat |
| **Lard** | 0% water | Pie crust, biscuits, tamales, frying | Flaky, savory, traditional Southern and Mexican |
| **Coconut oil** | 0% water | Vegan baking, tropical flavors | Solid when cold (like butter), melts at 76°F |
| **Oil (neutral)** | 0% water | Moist cakes, quick breads, muffins | Cannot cream (no air trapping), but very moist crumb |

### Temperature Matters

- **Cold fat** (40°F): for flaky pastry. Cold butter chunks melt in the oven,
  creating steam pockets = flakiness. If fat melts before the oven, no layers.
- **Room temp fat** (65–68°F): for creaming. Soft enough to trap air bubbles
  when beaten with sugar, firm enough to hold them.
- **Melted fat**: for dense, moist textures. Brownies, some cookies, oil cakes.
  No air trapping = denser crumb but more moisture.

## Sugar: More Than Sweetness

### What Sugar Does in Baking

| Function | Mechanism |
|----------|-----------|
| **Sweetness** | Obviously |
| **Moisture retention** | Sugar is hygroscopic — attracts and holds water, keeping baked goods moist for days |
| **Tenderness** | Competes with gluten for water, inhibiting gluten development |
| **Browning** | Caramelization (sugar alone, 320°F+) and Maillard reaction (sugar + protein, 280°F+) |
| **Spread** | In cookies, sugar dissolves and liquefies during baking, causing spread before structure sets |
| **Crust** | Sugar caramelizes on the surface, creating crisp exterior |
| **Creaming** | Granulated sugar's sharp crystals cut air pockets into butter |
| **Preservation** | High sugar concentration inhibits microbial growth |

### Sugar Types and Their Effects

| Sugar | Character | Baking Effect |
|-------|-----------|---------------|
| **Granulated (white)** | Neutral sweet, dry | Clean sweetness, good creaming, crisp cookies |
| **Brown sugar** | Molasses flavor, moist, acidic | Chewy cookies (hygroscopic), deeper flavor, reacts with baking soda |
| **Powdered (confectioners')** | Contains cornstarch, dissolves instantly | Frostings, delicate cookies (shortbread, Mexican wedding cookies) |
| **Honey** | Acidic, very hygroscopic, liquid | Moist baked goods, browns faster (reduce oven temp 25°F), acidic (add pinch of baking soda) |
| **Molasses** | Strongly acidic, intensely flavored, very hygroscopic | Gingerbread, dark cookies, reacts vigorously with baking soda |
| **Maple syrup** | Liquid, delicate flavor, moderate sweetness | Subtle flavor, reduce other liquids when substituting |
| **Coconut sugar** | Lower glycemic, caramel notes | Subs 1:1 for brown sugar in most recipes |

### The Cookie Spread Spectrum

This illustrates how ingredients interact — the same dough with small changes
produces completely different cookies:

| Want This | Do This |
|-----------|---------|
| **Flat & crispy** | More white sugar, melted butter, less flour, higher oven temp |
| **Chewy & thick** | More brown sugar, bread flour, less baking time, chill dough |
| **Cakey & puffy** | More flour, baking powder instead of soda, room temp butter (cream well) |

## Eggs: The Multi-Tool

### Egg Component Roles

| Part | What It Does | Temperature Notes |
|------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Whole egg** | Structure + moisture + richness + emulsification + leavening | Room temp eggs incorporate better into batter |
| **Yolk** | Fat + emulsifier (lecithin) + richness + color + tenderness | Custard base, enriched doughs, creme brulee |
| **White** | Structure + leavening when whipped + moisture | Whips best at room temp; fat kills foam |

### Egg Temperatures

- **Room temperature** eggs: for cakes and batters. Cold eggs can cause
  butter to seize, breaking the emulsion and producing a lumpy batter.
  Quick trick: submerge cold eggs in warm water for 10 minutes.
- **Cold eggs**: easier to separate (whites and yolks hold shape better)

### Egg Foams

Whipped egg whites create a protein foam that traps air. This foam is the
leavening in angel food cake, souffle, meringue, and macarons.

**Keys to a stable foam:**
- Bowl and whisk must be spotlessly clean — any fat kills the foam
- A pinch of cream of tartar (acid) stabilizes the protein network
- Add sugar gradually AFTER soft peaks — sugar strengthens the foam
  but slows whipping if added too early
- Don't overwhip — overwhipped whites break into clumps and weep liquid

### Egg Substitutes for Baking

| Substitute | Best For | How |
|-----------|----------|-----|
| **Flax egg** | Muffins, pancakes, cookies | 1 tbsp ground flax + 3 tbsp water, rest 5 min |
| **Chia egg** | Same as flax | 1 tbsp chia + 3 tbsp water, rest 5 min |
| **Aquafaba** | Meringue, macarons, mousse | 3 tbsp chickpea liquid = 1 egg white, whips like whites |
| **Applesauce** | Moist cakes, muffins | 1/4 cup = 1 egg (adds moisture and sweetness) |
| **Silken tofu** | Dense cakes, brownies | 1/4 cup blended = 1 egg |
| **Commercial replacer** | General baking | Follow package (Bob's Red Mill, JUST Egg) |

## Water and Hydration

### Hydration Ratios (Baker's Percentages)

In bread baking, all ingredients are expressed as a percentage of flour weight.
This makes scaling intuitive and reveals the character of the dough.

| Hydration | Character | Examples |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **50–57%** | Stiff, dense, tight crumb | Bagels, pretzels |
| **58–65%** | Standard, manageable | Sandwich bread, dinner rolls, enriched doughs |
| **65–75%** | Soft, open crumb, slightly sticky | French bread, ciabatta, focaccia |
| **75–85%** | Very wet, hard to handle, large holes | Sourdough boules, high-hydration ciabatta |
| **85%+** | Batter-like | Focaccia (no-knead style), some flatbreads |

### Steam in the Oven

Steam matters enormously in bread baking:
- Keeps the crust flexible during the first 10–15 minutes (oven spring)
- Gelatinizes surface starch, creating a glossy, crackly crust
- Professional bread ovens inject steam; home bakers use a Dutch oven
  (traps the dough's own steam) or a pan of boiling water

## Temperature and Time

### Oven Temperature Guide

| Temperature | What It Does | Use For |
|-------------|-------------|---------|
| **300–325°F** | Gentle, even baking, minimal browning | Custards, cheesecake, delicate cakes, meringues |
| **350°F** | The universal default — balanced browning and cooking | Most cakes, cookies, muffins, quick breads |
| **375–400°F** | Faster browning, more oven spring | Biscuits, scones, pies, rustic breads |
| **425–475°F** | Rapid crust formation, maximum steam | Artisan bread, pizza, puff pastry, popovers |
| **500°F+** | Extreme crust, Neapolitan pizza | Pizza steel/stone (short bake, 5–7 min) |

### The Carryover Effect

Baked goods continue cooking from residual heat after leaving the oven.
- Cookies firm up significantly as they cool (take them out when slightly
  underdone in the center for a chewy result)
- Bread's internal temperature can rise 5–10°F after removal
- Cakes should be cooled in the pan for 10 minutes before turning out
  (structure needs time to set)

### Doneness Cues

| Baked Good | Visual/Tactile Cue | Internal Temp |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------|
| **Bread** | Hollow thump when tapped on bottom | 190–210°F |
| **Cake** | Springs back when pressed, pulls from sides | 200–210°F |
| **Cookies** | Edges set, center looks slightly underdone | N/A — go by look |
| **Pie crust** | Deep golden, not pale | N/A |
| **Custard/cheesecake** | Center jiggles (not waves) when shaken | 170–175°F |
| **Quick bread** | Toothpick comes out with moist crumbs, not wet batter | 200°F |

## Common Baking Failures and Fixes

| Problem | Likely Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Cake is dense and heavy | Overmixed (too much gluten), or under-creamed butter/sugar | Mix dry ingredients until JUST combined; cream butter + sugar 3–5 min until fluffy and pale |
| Cake sinks in the center | Underbaked, too much leavening, or oven opened too early | Test doneness with a toothpick; reduce baking powder; don't open oven until last 5 min |
| Cookies spread flat | Butter too warm, not enough flour, too much sugar | Chill dough 30 min; measure flour by weight; reduce sugar slightly |
| Cookies are cakey | Too much flour, too many eggs, baking powder instead of soda | Reduce flour by 2 tbsp; use 1 yolk instead of 1 whole egg |
| Bread didn't rise | Yeast killed by hot liquid, or expired yeast | Water must be 105–110°F (not hotter); test yeast by proofing first |
| Bread is dense | Not enough kneading, or not enough rising time | Knead to windowpane; let dough double (poke test — indent holds) |
| Pie crust is tough | Overworked dough (too much gluten) | Handle less; keep fat cold; use pastry flour or add 1 tsp vinegar |
| Pie crust shrinks | Dough was stretched (not relaxed), or not rested | Always rest dough 30 min in fridge before rolling; don't stretch into pan |
| Muffins have tunnels | Overmixed batter | Stir until just barely combined — lumps are OK |
| Biscuits aren't flaky | Fat melted before baking, or dough overworked | Freeze butter, grate it in; fold dough 3–4 times only; work fast |

## Ratios Worth Memorizing

These ratios (by weight) are the skeletons of baking. Once you know them,
you can improvise:

| Product | Ratio (by weight) | Notes |
|---------|-------------------|-------|
| **Basic bread** | 5 flour : 3 water : 0.1 salt : 0.05 yeast | The simplest bread possible |
| **Pie dough** | 3 flour : 2 fat : 1 water | Keep everything cold |
| **Biscuits** | 3 flour : 1 fat : 1 liquid | Self-rising flour + cold butter + buttermilk |
| **Muffin** | 2 flour : 1 liquid : 1 egg : fat + sugar to taste | The "muffin method" — wet into dry |
| **Pound cake** | 1 flour : 1 butter : 1 sugar : 1 egg | Equal parts — the original ratio |
| **Cookie (basic)** | 3 flour : 2 fat : 1 sugar | Adjust sugar type and ratio for texture |
| **Choux paste** | 1 water : 1 butter : 1 flour : 2 eggs | Cook on stovetop first, then bake |
| **Crepe** | 1 flour : 1 egg : 1 milk | Thin, no leavening |

These are starting points. Every great recipe is a riff on a ratio.
