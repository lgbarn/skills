# Cooking Techniques Reference

Detailed guide to cooking methods organized by type with cuisine context.
Load this file when a user needs guidance on a specific technique, or when
building a recipe that relies on technique-dependent outcomes.

---

## Dry Heat Methods

### Grilling
**Principle**: radiant heat from below creates intense direct-contact browning.

**When to use**: steaks, burgers, kebabs, vegetables, flatbreads, whole fish
**Key tips**:
- Two-zone fire: hot side for searing, cool side for finishing
- Oil the grate, not the food (reduces flare-ups)
- Only flip once — let the crust develop fully
- Use a meat thermometer, not time, for doneness
**Common mistakes**: moving food too often, overcrowding, no temperature zones
**Cuisine context**: Nigerian suya, Jamaican jerk, Argentine asado, Korean gogigui,
Turkish kebap, American BBQ, Japanese yakitori

### Roasting
**Principle**: dry oven heat surrounds food, producing even browning through
Maillard reaction and caramelization.

**When to use**: whole chickens, root vegetables, large cuts of meat, squash
**Key tips**:
- Pat food dry (moisture inhibits browning)
- High heat (400–450°F) for crispy exteriors
- Lower heat (300–325°F) for gentle cooking of large cuts
- Elevate on a rack for air circulation
- Rest meat after roasting (juices redistribute, 10–15 minutes)
**Common mistakes**: not preheating oven, crowding the pan, skipping rest time
**Cuisine context**: French rotisserie, British Sunday roast, Middle Eastern shawarma,
Ethiopian tibs, Peruvian pollo a la brasa

### Searing / Pan-Frying
**Principle**: high heat + small amount of fat creates rapid Maillard browning
on the food's surface.

**When to use**: steaks, chops, fish fillets, scallops, skin-on chicken
**Key tips**:
- Pan must be hot BEFORE adding fat
- Fat must shimmer/ripple BEFORE adding food
- Do NOT crowd the pan (creates steam instead of sear)
- Pat protein dry before searing
- Don't move it — let the crust form before flipping
**Common mistakes**: cold pan, wet protein, overcrowding, flipping too early
**Cuisine context**: French pan sauces, Italian scaloppine, soul food smothered chops

### Sauteing / Stir-Frying
**Principle**: high heat, small amount of fat, food in constant motion.

**When to use**: diced vegetables, shrimp, thin-sliced protein, aromatics
**Key tips**:
- Cut food to uniform size for even cooking
- Everything prepped (mise en place) before heat goes on — this cooks fast
- Wok: heat until smoking, add oil, immediately add food
- Small batches to maintain high heat
**Common mistakes**: uneven cuts, too much food at once, low heat, wet vegetables
**Cuisine context**: Chinese wok hei, French sauter, Italian saltare in padella,
Thai pad, soul food quick-cooked okra

### Deep Frying
**Principle**: full submersion in hot oil creates rapid browning and moisture seal.

**When to use**: chicken, fish, fritters, donuts, vegetables (tempura), dumplings
**Key tips**:
- Oil temperature: 325–375°F depending on item (lower for thick/bone-in, higher for thin)
- USE A THERMOMETER — temperature drops when food goes in, must recover
- Do not overcrowd (drops temp dramatically, creates steam)
- Drain on wire rack, NOT paper towels (steam trapped underneath softens crust)
- Season immediately after frying (seasoning sticks to hot oil surface)
**Common mistakes**: no thermometer, overcrowding, paper towel draining, cold oil
**Cuisine context**: soul food fried chicken and catfish, Japanese tempura and tonkatsu,
West African akara and fried plantain, Indian pakora and samosa, Korean fried chicken

### Smoking
**Principle**: low temperature (200–275°F) + wood smoke over extended time.
Smoke phenolic compounds (guaiacol, syringol) add flavor and preserve.

**When to use**: brisket, ribs, pork shoulder, sausage, fish, chicken
**Key tips**:
- **Wood selection matters**:
  - Hickory: strong, bacon-like (pork ribs, Southern BBQ)
  - Oak: medium, versatile (brisket, beef)
  - Applewood: mild, sweet (poultry, pork)
  - Mesquite: intense, use sparingly (Tex-Mex)
  - Pecan: mild, nutty (popular in Southern smoking)
  - Cherry: mild, fruity, beautiful mahogany color
- Maintain consistent low temperature
- Spritz with apple cider vinegar or juice for moisture
- The "stall" at 150–160°F is normal (collagen breaking down — push through it)
- Rest 30+ minutes after smoking
**Common mistakes**: too much smoke (bitter), temperature swings, no rest, wrong wood
**Cuisine context**: soul food/BBQ (ribs, pulled pork, brisket), West African smoked fish,
Scandinavian cold-smoking, Chinese tea-smoking

## Moist Heat Methods

### Braising
**Principle**: sear in dry heat, then cook low and slow in a small amount of
liquid in a covered vessel. Converts tough collagen to silky gelatin.

**When to use**: tough cuts (short ribs, oxtails, shoulder, shanks), greens
**Key tips**:
- Sear WELL first (deep brown, not gray — this is where flavor compounds form)
- Liquid should come 1/3 to 1/2 up the food, NOT fully submerged
- Low temperature: 300–325°F oven or lowest stovetop setting
- Time is the ingredient: 2–4 hours for tough cuts
- Cool and refrigerate overnight — flavors meld and improve dramatically
**Common mistakes**: inadequate searing, too much liquid (stewing not braising),
too high heat (tough instead of tender), not enough time
**Cuisine context**: soul food oxtails and smothered pork chops, French coq au vin
and boeuf bourguignon, Italian osso buco, Ethiopian wot, Mexican barbacoa and birria

### Stewing
**Principle**: food fully submerged in liquid, cooked at a gentle simmer.
Flavors exchange between liquid and solids over time.

**When to use**: beans, tough meats in pieces, soups, chili, gumbo
**Key tips**:
- NEVER boil — gentle simmer only (small bubbles breaking surface)
- Build layers: sear meat, sweat aromatics, deglaze, add liquid
- Add delicate vegetables later so they don't overcook
- Season at end (liquid reduces and concentrates, oversalting early is common)
**Common mistakes**: boiling (makes meat tough), adding everything at once,
seasoning too early, not skimming foam
**Cuisine context**: soul food red beans, gumbo; West African groundnut soup, egusi;
Indian dal; Caribbean curry goat; Japanese nikujaga

### Steaming
**Principle**: gentle moist heat from steam cooks food without submersion.
Preserves nutrients, texture, and delicate flavors.

**When to use**: fish, dumplings, delicate vegetables, custards, buns
**Key tips**:
- Water must be at full boil before food goes in
- Do not let food touch the water
- Leave space between items for steam circulation
- Do not lift the lid unnecessarily (releases steam, drops temperature)
**Common mistakes**: food touching water, overcrowding, lid-lifting
**Cuisine context**: Chinese dim sum and steamed fish, Japanese chawanmushi,
Thai steamed curry in banana leaf, tamales

### Poaching
**Principle**: cooking gently in liquid held just below a simmer (160–180°F).
Liquid barely trembles.

**When to use**: eggs, delicate fish, chicken breast, fruit
**Key tips**:
- Temperature control is everything — no bubbles, just gentle movement
- Flavor the poaching liquid (court bouillon, stock, aromatics, wine)
- The poaching liquid can become a sauce
- Acid in liquid (vinegar for eggs) helps protein set quickly
**Common mistakes**: water too hot (tough, flaky protein), bland poaching liquid
**Cuisine context**: French poached eggs and poached pear, Chinese white-cut chicken,
Japanese shabu-shabu

### Boiling / Simmering
**Principle**: full submersion in liquid at or near 212°F. Simmering (185–205°F)
is gentler than a rolling boil.

**When to use**: pasta, grains, potatoes, blanching vegetables, stocks
**Key tips**:
- Salt pasta water generously ("like the sea")
- Blanch vegetables in salted boiling water, then ice bath (shock)
- Stocks simmer, never boil (boiling makes stock cloudy)
- Start root vegetables in cold water, green vegetables in boiling water
**Common mistakes**: unsalted water, no ice bath, boiling stocks
**Cuisine context**: Italian pasta, Japanese dashi, French blanching, soul food boiled peanuts

## Preparation Techniques

### Marinating
**Principle**: soaking food in an acidic, enzymatic, or salt-based liquid to
tenderize and add flavor from the outside in.

**Components**: acid (citrus, vinegar, wine, yogurt) + oil + aromatics
**Timing**:
- Fish/shrimp: 15–30 minutes max (acid "cooks" protein)
- Chicken: 2–24 hours
- Beef/pork: 4–24 hours
- Yogurt-based (tandoori): 4–48 hours (gentle acid)
**Cuisine context**: Indian tandoori (yogurt), Caribbean jerk (allspice-scotch bonnet),
Korean bulgogi (soy-sugar-pear), Peruvian ceviche (lime "cooking")

### Brining
**Principle**: salt solution (wet brine) or salt coating (dry brine) denatures
proteins, allowing meat to retain 10–15% more moisture during cooking.

**Wet brine**: 1/4 cup salt per quart of water, submerge 4–24 hours
**Dry brine**: coat with salt, refrigerate uncovered 12–48 hours
**Key tip**: dry brine is generally superior — concentrates flavor, crisps skin
**Cuisine context**: soul food fried chicken (buttermilk brine), Thanksgiving turkey,
Chinese soy-brined eggs, Jewish deli corned beef

### Curing
**Principle**: salt and/or sugar draws moisture out, preserving and concentrating flavor.
Often combined with smoke or air-drying.

**Types**: salt curing, sugar curing, nitrate curing (pink salt)
**Cuisine context**: Italian prosciutto and pancetta, Chinese lap cheong, soul food
salt pork and country ham, Scandinavian gravlax, Middle Eastern basterma

### Fermenting
**Principle**: microbial transformation creates new flavor compounds, preserves food,
develops umami and complexity.

**Types**:
- **Lactic fermentation**: kimchi, sauerkraut, pickles, hot sauce, yogurt
- **Mold fermentation**: miso, soy sauce, tempeh, blue cheese
- **Yeast fermentation**: bread, beer, wine, injera
- **Acetic fermentation**: vinegar
**Cuisine context**: Korean kimchi and doenjang, Japanese miso and soy sauce,
Ethiopian injera, West African dawadawa and ogiri, European cheese and sourdough

### Pickling
**Principle**: acid (vinegar) or fermentation preserves food and adds tang.

**Quick pickle**: vinegar + sugar + salt brine, ready in 30 minutes
**Fermented pickle**: salt brine only, lacto-fermentation over days/weeks
**Cuisine context**: Southern chow-chow and pickled okra, Japanese tsukemono,
Korean banchan, Indian achaar, Middle Eastern turnip pickles

## Spice Techniques

### Toasting Whole Spices
**Principle**: dry heat releases volatile oils trapped in seed coats.
Transforms raw/harsh flavors into warm/complex ones.

**Method**: dry skillet over medium heat, 1–3 minutes, shaking frequently,
until fragrant and slightly darkened. Grind immediately after cooling.
**Cuisine context**: Indian (cumin, coriander, fennel before grinding), Mexican
(dried chilies on comal), Ethiopian (berbere spice component toasting)

### Blooming in Oil (Tadka / Tempering)
**Principle**: hot fat extracts and disperses fat-soluble flavor compounds
from spices, distributing them evenly throughout the dish.

**Method**: heat oil/ghee until hot (not smoking), add whole spices in order
of cooking time (hard seeds first, leaves last):
- Cumin seeds: 15–20 seconds until sizzling and one shade darker
- Mustard seeds: until they pop
- Curry leaves: add last, they splatter
**Cuisine context**: Indian tadka/chaunk/baghaar, Ethiopian niter kibbeh base,
Middle Eastern blooming spices in olive oil, soul food blooming paprika in bacon fat

### Building Spice Pastes
**Principle**: grinding fresh aromatics and spices into a wet paste creates
intense, cohesive flavor base.

**Methods**: mortar and pestle (preferred — bruising releases more oils than
blending), food processor, blender with liquid
**Cuisine context**: Thai curry pastes, Mexican mole and adobo, North African
chermoula and harissa, Indonesian bumbu, Malaysian rempah

### Dry Rubs
**Principle**: mix of ground spices and salt applied directly to protein.
Salt draws out moisture, dissolves, and is reabsorbed carrying spice flavors.

**Timing**: apply 1–24 hours before cooking for penetration
**Cuisine context**: BBQ rubs (paprika, brown sugar, garlic, cumin), Jamaican
jerk dry rub, Ethiopian mitmita, soul food fried chicken seasoned flour

### Coffee as a Culinary Spice
**Principle**: coffee is a spice, not just a beverage. Its roasted compounds
(pyrazines, furanones, guaiacol) add bitter complexity, savory depth, and
amplify other flavors — especially chocolate, smoke, and caramelized meat.
Coffee works in cooking the same way vanilla does: often invisible as itself,
but transformative in what it does to everything around it.

**Forms and when to use each:**

| Form | Flavor Character | Best For | Notes |
|------|-----------------|----------|-------|
| **Espresso powder (instant)** | Concentrated, clean, dissolves instantly | Baking (brownies, chocolate cake, ganache), sauces, glazes | The most versatile form — a teaspoon amplifies chocolate without tasting like coffee |
| **Finely ground coffee** | Gritty texture, bold, aromatic | Dry rubs, spice crusts, chili | Grind fine (almost powder) for rubs; coarse grinds are unpleasant on the tongue |
| **Brewed coffee (strong)** | Liquid, bitter, complex | Braising liquid, deglazing, marinades, sauces | Use strong brew or espresso; weak coffee adds bitterness without depth |
| **Whole beans (crushed)** | Aromatic, textural | Infusions, coarse rubs (removed before eating), spirit infusions | Lightly crush, steep, strain — like using whole peppercorns |
| **Cold brew concentrate** | Smooth, sweet-leaning, low acid | Sauces, glazes, dessert sauces, BBQ mop | Less bitter than hot brew; good for sweet applications |

**How much to use:**
- **As a background amplifier** (baking, chili): 1–2 teaspoons espresso powder
  per recipe. You shouldn't taste "coffee" — you should taste deeper, richer
  chocolate or more complex chili.
- **As a noticeable flavor** (rubs, glazes, tiramisu): 1–2 tablespoons ground
  coffee or espresso powder. Coffee is a stated participant.
- **As a primary ingredient** (coffee BBQ sauce, red-eye gravy, affogato):
  1/2 cup+ brewed coffee. Coffee is the star.

**Key applications:**

**In dry rubs (BBQ and grilling):**
Finely ground coffee + brown sugar + smoked paprika + black pepper + garlic
powder + salt. The coffee's bitterness balances the sugar's sweetness, and
its roasted compounds (guaiacol, pyrazines) harmonize with the smoke from
the grill. Classic on brisket, pork shoulder, and ribs. Apply 1–12 hours
before cooking.

**In chocolate baking:**
Add 1–2 teaspoons espresso powder to any chocolate baked good — brownies,
cake, ganache, mousse, hot chocolate. Coffee and chocolate share pyrazines
and furanones from the roasting process (both are roasted seeds). The coffee
doesn't make it taste like mocha; it makes the chocolate taste more intensely
like itself. This is one of the most reliable tricks in baking.

**In chili and stews:**
A tablespoon of espresso powder or a shot of strong coffee added to chili,
beef stew, or bolognese deepens the savory base. Coffee's bitterness rounds
out the sweetness of tomatoes and adds a dark, roasted complexity that reads
as "depth" rather than "coffee." Add early, with the liquid, so it integrates.

**In braising and deglazing (red-eye gravy):**
Southern red-eye gravy: fry country ham, deglaze the pan with strong black
coffee. The coffee lifts the fond (caramelized meat sugars) and combines with
the ham's smoky, salty drippings. Served over ham, biscuits, or grits. The
bitterness of coffee + the salt of ham + the sweetness of caramelized fond
= a complete flavor triangle.

**In marinades and glazes:**
Coffee-based marinades work on beef and pork — the acids in coffee gently
tenderize the surface while depositing roasted flavor. Coffee + brown sugar +
soy sauce + garlic = an excellent steak or short rib marinade. For glazes,
reduce strong coffee with brown sugar, a splash of vinegar, and spices into
a syrupy BBQ glaze.

**In desserts beyond baking:**
Tiramisu (espresso-soaked ladyfingers), affogato (espresso over ice cream),
coffee ice cream, coffee panna cotta, espresso caramel sauce. Coffee's
bitterness is the counterweight to sugar and cream in rich desserts — it
prevents sweetness from becoming cloying.

**Cuisine context**: Southern red-eye gravy, Texas-style coffee brisket rubs,
Mexican mole (some regional versions), Ethiopian coffee-spiced dishes,
Vietnamese egg coffee, Italian tiramisu and espresso desserts, Middle Eastern
cardamom coffee in sweets

## Sauce Building

### Roux-Based Sauces
**Principle**: equal parts fat + flour cooked together, then liquid added.
Color determines flavor and thickening power.

| Stage | Color | Time | Flavor | Thickening | Use |
|-------|-------|------|--------|------------|-----|
| White roux | Pale | 2–3 min | Neutral | Strongest | Bechamel, white gravy |
| Blond roux | Gold | 5–7 min | Slightly nutty | Strong | Veloute, cream sauces |
| Brown roux | Peanut butter | 15–20 min | Deeply nutty | Medium | Gumbo, etouffee |
| Dark roux | Chocolate | 30–45 min | Intense, smoky | Weakest | Dark gumbo, Creole |

**Key**: constant stirring, medium heat, patience. Darker = more flavor, LESS thickening.
**Cuisine context**: French mother sauces, soul food/Creole gumbo, Southern white gravy

### Reduction Sauces
**Principle**: simmering liquid to evaporate water, concentrating flavors and
thickening through natural gelatin and sugar.

**Pan sauce method**: sear protein → remove → deglaze with wine/stock → reduce →
finish with butter (monte au beurre) or cream
**Cuisine context**: French classical cuisine, Italian wine reductions

### Emulsion Sauces
**Principle**: dispersing fat in water (or vice versa) with an emulsifier
(egg yolk, mustard, lecithin) to create creamy, stable sauces.

**Types**:
- **Hot emulsion**: hollandaise, bearnaise (egg yolk + butter)
- **Cold emulsion**: mayonnaise, vinaigrette, aioli (egg yolk/mustard + oil)
- **Starch emulsion**: pasta water + fat + cheese (cacio e pepe, alfredo)
**Common mistakes**: too-hot butter breaks hollandaise, adding oil too fast breaks mayo
**Cuisine context**: French hollandaise, Italian pasta emulsions, Middle Eastern tahini

### Compound Butters
**Principle**: softened butter mixed with herbs, spices, or aromatics.
Melts on contact with hot food, creating instant sauce.

**Examples**: garlic-herb butter, cafe de Paris butter, maître d'hôtel butter
**Cuisine context**: French classical, steakhouse finishing, compound butters on cornbread

## Technique Selection Guide

When choosing a cooking method, match ingredient type to desired outcome:

| Ingredient Type | Tender Result | Crispy Result | Rich/Deep Result |
|----------------|---------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Tough meat (shoulder, oxtail) | Braise 3–4 hrs | Braise then broil/fry | Smoke 8–12 hrs |
| Tender meat (loin, breast) | Poach or steam | Saute or grill | Roast at high heat |
| Root vegetables | Steam or simmer | Roast at 425°F | Braise in stock |
| Leafy greens | Quick saute (2 min) | Chip (kale, 350°F) | Braise with smoked pork |
| Beans / legumes | Simmer until creamy | Fry cooked beans (crispy) | Slow-cook with aromatics |
| Fish | Poach or steam | Pan-fry or deep-fry | Smoke or en papillote |
| Dough / batter | Steam (dumplings) | Deep fry (fritters) | Bake low (custard) |
| Eggs | Poach or soft-boil | Fry in hot oil | Slow-scramble with butter |
