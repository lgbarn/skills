# Cooking Science Reference

Scientific reference for why cooking techniques transform food. Load this file
when a user asks WHY a technique works, wants to troubleshoot a cooking
problem, or is curious about the science behind a method. This complements
flavor-science.md (which covers why flavors pair) by explaining what happens
to food at a molecular level during cooking.

## Protein Science

### Denaturation: What Happens When Proteins Cook

Proteins are coiled chains of amino acids. Heat causes them to unwind
(denature) and bond with each other in new arrangements. This is why a raw
egg becomes solid, a steak becomes firm, and a piece of fish turns from
translucent to opaque.

**The critical insight**: different proteins denature at different temperatures.
Understanding these thresholds is the key to perfectly cooked meat, eggs, and
custards.

### Meat Protein Temperature Thresholds

| Temperature | What Happens | What You See |
|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| **105°F / 40°C** | Myosin (the main muscle protein) begins to denature | Meat starts to firm very slightly |
| **120°F / 49°C** | Myosin denaturation accelerates | Rare steak — red center, warm, tender |
| **130–140°F / 54–60°C** | Myosin is fully denatured; collagen begins to contract | Medium-rare to medium — pink, juicy, firmer bite |
| **150°F / 66°C** | Collagen contracts sharply, squeezing out moisture | Medium-well — the "danger zone" for dry meat |
| **160°F / 71°C** | Actin (the other major protein) denatures | Well-done — gray, dry, tough. But also the temp where collagen starts converting to gelatin in long cooks |
| **160–180°F / 71–82°C** (sustained) | Collagen → gelatin conversion over hours | Braised brisket, pulled pork — tough becomes silky |
| **180°F+ / 82°C+** (sustained) | Gelatin fully dissolves, fat renders completely | Fall-off-the-bone ribs, pot roast |

**Why this matters for cooking method selection:**
- Quick-cook (grill, sear, saute) for tender cuts: get to 130°F internal
  fast, stop there. The meat was never tough to begin with.
- Long-cook (braise, smoke, stew) for tough cuts: push past the "dry zone"
  (140–160°F) by cooking for hours at low heat until collagen converts to
  gelatin at 160–180°F. The gelatin replaces lost moisture with silky richness.
- The worst outcome: cooking a tough cut quickly to 160°F. Collagen hasn't
  had time to convert, actin is fully denatured = maximum toughness + dryness.

### Carryover Cooking

Meat continues cooking after removal from heat. The exterior is hotter than
the center, and that heat migrates inward:

| Cut Size | Expected Carryover | Pull Temperature for Medium-Rare |
|----------|-------------------|--------------------------------|
| Steak (1–1.5 inch) | +5°F | Pull at 125°F → rests to 130°F |
| Roast (3–5 lbs) | +10–15°F | Pull at 120°F → rests to 130–135°F |
| Whole turkey/chicken | +10°F | Pull at 155°F → rests to 165°F |

**Resting**: when you rest meat, the temperature equalizes and the protein
matrix relaxes, reabsorbing some of the squeezed-out juices. Cut into a
steak immediately and the juices flood the plate. Rest 5–10 minutes and
those juices stay in the meat.

### Egg Protein Behavior

Eggs are the cook's most precise protein tool. Each component sets at
different temperatures:

| Component | Sets At | Result |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| **Egg white (outer)** | 180°F / 82°C | Firm, fully opaque white |
| **Egg white (inner, near yolk)** | 145°F / 63°C | Barely set, tender |
| **Egg yolk** | 149°F / 65°C | Begins to thicken |
| **Egg yolk** | 158°F / 70°C | Crumbly, fully set |
| **Custard (yolk + liquid)** | 170–175°F / 77–79°C | Thickened but pourable; above 185°F = curdled scrambled eggs in your creme anglaise |

**Practical applications:**
- Soft-boiled egg: 6–7 minutes in boiling water (or 145°F sous vide for
  45 min for a uniformly jammy yolk)
- Scrambled eggs low-and-slow: keep heat gentle so yolks set into large,
  creamy curds instead of tight, rubbery ones
- Custard: temper the eggs (add hot liquid slowly while whisking) to raise
  their temperature gradually without scrambling

## Collagen and Gelatin

### The Toughness Paradox

Collagen is the connective tissue in meat — it's tough, chewy, and inedible
when raw. But given enough heat and time, it converts to gelatin, which is
silky, rich, and gives body to sauces and braising liquids.

**Conversion requirements:**
- Temperature: sustained 160°F+ (ideally 180–205°F)
- Time: 2–4+ hours depending on cut and temperature
- Moisture: helps, which is why braising and stewing are the go-to methods

**Where collagen lives:**
- Shoulder (chuck, pork butt): high collagen → great for braising, pulled pork
- Shanks (osso buco, lamb shank): very high collagen → braise until luscious
- Oxtail: extremely high collagen → the richest braising liquid of any cut
- Ribs: moderate collagen → smoking or braising
- Skin and feet: nearly pure collagen → stock and bone broth

**Why stock made from bones gels when cold**: the dissolved gelatin is a
hydrocolloid — it forms a gel at refrigerator temperatures. This gel is pure
body and richness. If your stock doesn't gel, it didn't cook long enough or
didn't have enough collagen-rich bones.

### Practical Rules

- Tough cut + quick cook = inedible
- Tough cut + long slow cook = incredible
- Tender cut + quick cook = perfect
- Tender cut + long slow cook = dry mush (no collagen to save it)

## Starch Science

### Gelatinization: How Starch Thickens

Starch granules absorb water and swell when heated, eventually bursting and
releasing their starch molecules into the liquid. This thickens sauces, gravies,
and custards.

| Starch | Gelatinization Temp | Thickening Power | Character | Best For |
|--------|-------------------|-----------------|-----------|----------|
| **Cornstarch** | 144–180°F | Strong | Clear, glossy, breaks down with prolonged heat | Stir-fry sauces, pie fillings, Chinese cooking |
| **Flour (roux)** | 140–160°F | Moderate | Opaque, matte, stable over long cooking | Gumbo, gravy, bechamel, chowder |
| **Tapioca** | 140–170°F | Strong | Very clear, slightly chewy, glossy | Pie fillings, bubble tea, GF baking |
| **Arrowroot** | 140–170°F | Strong | Crystal clear, breaks if overheated or over-stirred | Delicate sauces, fruit glazes |
| **Potato starch** | 136–170°F | Very strong | Clear, can get gluey if overworked | Last-minute thickening, GF baking |

### The Roux Spectrum

A roux (equal parts fat + flour by weight) is the most common thickener in
Western and Southern cooking. The color determines both flavor and power:

| Color | Cook Time | Flavor | Thickening Power |
|-------|-----------|--------|-----------------|
| **White** | 2–3 min | Neutral, raw flour cooked off | Strongest |
| **Blond** | 5–8 min | Slightly nutty | Strong |
| **Peanut butter** | 15–20 min | Nutty, toasty | Medium |
| **Chocolate (dark)** | 30–45 min | Intense, smoky, deeply complex | Weakest |

**Why dark roux thickens less**: prolonged heat breaks down the starch molecules.
What you lose in thickening, you gain in flavor. Soul food and Creole gumbo
use dark roux for flavor, then rely on okra or file powder for additional body.

### Starch Retrogradation

When cooked starches cool, they re-crystallize (retrograde), becoming firmer
and sometimes gummy. This is why:
- Day-old bread is stale (starch retrogradation, not moisture loss — toast it
  to re-gelatinize)
- Leftover rice is firm (use it for fried rice — the firm grains fry better)
- Mashed potatoes stiffen in the fridge (reheat gently with added liquid)
- Potato salad tastes better the next day (starch retrogradation creates a
  firmer, more pleasant texture, and the dressing has time to penetrate)

## Emulsion Science

### What Is an Emulsion?

An emulsion is a stable mixture of two liquids that don't normally mix (oil
and water). One liquid is dispersed as tiny droplets in the other. An
emulsifier holds them together.

### Types of Culinary Emulsions

| Type | Structure | Examples |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Oil-in-water** | Tiny oil droplets in water phase | Vinaigrette, mayonnaise, hollandaise, milk, cream soups |
| **Water-in-oil** | Tiny water droplets in oil phase | Butter, chocolate ganache, some pan sauces |

### Key Emulsifiers

| Emulsifier | Found In | How It Works |
|-----------|----------|-------------|
| **Lecithin** | Egg yolks, soy | One end bonds to water, other to fat — bridges the gap |
| **Casein** | Milk, cream | Protein that stabilizes fat droplets in dairy |
| **Mustard** | Dijon, whole grain | Mucilage and particles create physical stabilization |
| **Starch** | Pasta water, roux | Thickens the water phase, making droplets harder to merge |
| **Gelatin** | Stock, meat juices | Protein network traps fat droplets |

### Making and Rescuing Emulsions

**Building a stable emulsion:**
1. Start with the water-based ingredient
2. Add fat SLOWLY — a thin stream while whisking constantly
3. Temperature helps: warm (not hot) ingredients emulsify more easily
4. The first few drops are critical — go drop by drop until the emulsion
   "takes" (thickens slightly), then you can add fat faster

**Fixing a broken emulsion:**
- **Mayonnaise broke**: start a new yolk in a clean bowl, drizzle the broken
  mayo into it slowly while whisking
- **Hollandaise broke**: add 1 tablespoon ice water and whisk vigorously; or
  start with a fresh yolk and drizzle broken sauce in
- **Vinaigrette separated**: re-whisk with a pinch of mustard (physical emulsifier)
- **Pan sauce oily**: add a splash of stock or water off heat, swirl vigorously

### Pasta Water: The Emulsifier You Already Have

Starchy pasta cooking water is a natural emulsifier. When you toss hot pasta
with fat (olive oil, butter, cheese, rendered meat fat) and a splash of pasta
water, the starch bridges the water and fat into a creamy sauce. This is the
entire basis of:
- Cacio e pepe (pecorino + pepper + pasta water)
- Aglio e olio (garlic + olive oil + pasta water)
- Carbonara (egg + pecorino + guanciale fat + pasta water)

**The trick**: reserve pasta water BEFORE draining. Use the starchiest water
(from the last minutes of cooking). Add gradually while tossing aggressively
over medium heat.

## pH and Cooking

### Why Acid and Alkaline Matter

The pH scale (0–14) affects color, texture, flavor, and chemical reactions
in cooking. Most foods fall between pH 3 (acidic) and pH 8 (slightly alkaline).

### Practical pH Effects

| pH Change | Effect | Application |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| **Adding acid (lower pH)** | Slows browning | Add lemon juice AFTER searing, not before |
| **Adding acid** | Preserves green color (briefly) | Splash of lemon keeps sauteed greens bright |
| **Adding acid** | Tenderizes protein surfaces | Marinades (citrus, vinegar, wine, yogurt) |
| **Adding acid** | Activates baking soda | Buttermilk + baking soda = rise |
| **Adding acid** | Helps pectin gel | Jam and jelly need acid + sugar + pectin |
| **Adding alkaline (higher pH)** | Accelerates Maillard browning | Baking soda on pretzels, in cookie dough |
| **Adding alkaline** | Softens cell walls | Baking soda in bean cooking water (faster cook) |
| **Adding alkaline** | Changes noodle texture | Kansui (alkaline salts) gives ramen noodles their springy chew and yellow color |
| **Adding alkaline** | Turns anthocyanins blue/green | Red cabbage + baking soda turns blue-green |
| **Adding alkaline** | Dissolves surface proteins | Pretzels dipped in lye solution for their crust |

### Color and pH

Anthocyanins (pigments in red cabbage, blueberries, radishes) are natural
pH indicators:
- Acid = red/pink (red cabbage with vinegar stays red)
- Neutral = purple
- Alkaline = blue/green

Chlorophyll (green pigment) in vegetables:
- Acid destroys it over time (long-cooked greens turn olive-drab)
- Brief cooking preserves it (blanch and shock for vivid green)
- Alkaline preserves it but makes texture mushy (don't add baking soda to
  green vegetables — they'll be bright green but unpleasantly soft)

## Osmosis, Diffusion, and Salt

### How Salt Moves Through Food

Salt doesn't just sit on the surface — it moves inward through osmosis and
diffusion. But it's SLOW. This is why:

| Timing | Penetration | Best For |
|--------|------------|----------|
| **Surface seasoning (immediate)** | Surface only | Searing, grilling, finishing |
| **30 min – 1 hour** | ~1/4 inch | Steaks, chops, fish |
| **4–12 hours (dry brine)** | Deep, throughout | Chicken, turkey, pork roasts |
| **12–48 hours** | Full penetration | Large roasts, whole birds |

### Dry Brine vs. Wet Brine

| Method | How | Why It Works | Pros | Cons |
|--------|-----|-------------|------|------|
| **Dry brine** | Coat with salt, rest uncovered in fridge | Salt draws out surface moisture, dissolves in it, then is reabsorbed along with flavor. Surface dries = better browning | Better browning, concentrated flavor, easy, no mess | Slower |
| **Wet brine** | Submerge in salt water solution | Osmosis pulls salt water into the meat, increasing moisture content by 10–15% | Very juicy meat, works faster | Can taste waterlogged; dilutes flavor; messy |

**Modern consensus**: dry brine is almost always better. It gives you the
seasoning penetration of brining with the surface drying that produces
superior browning. The only exception is very lean meats (turkey breast,
chicken breast) where the extra moisture from a wet brine is welcome insurance.

### Why Salting Vegetables Draws Water

Salt creates a concentration gradient. Water moves from inside the cell
(low salt) to the surface (high salt) via osmosis. This is useful for:
- **Eggplant**: salt and rest 30 min, pat dry — removes bitterness and excess
  moisture so it fries instead of steams
- **Cucumbers**: salt for quick pickles, or to draw water for tzatziki
- **Cabbage**: salt for sauerkraut — the drawn-out water becomes the brine
- **Tomatoes**: salt before adding to salad — concentrates flavor, prevents
  watery dressing

## Heat Transfer Methods

### Why Cooking Method Matters

Different methods transfer heat to food at dramatically different rates,
which is why a steak cooks in minutes on a grill but takes hours in an oven.

| Method | Medium | Heat Transfer Rate | Best For |
|--------|--------|-------------------|----------|
| **Conduction** | Direct contact (pan, griddle) | Very fast | Searing, pan-frying, griddling |
| **Convection** | Moving air or liquid | Moderate | Roasting, baking, boiling, simmering |
| **Radiation** | Infrared energy (grill, broiler) | Fast at surface | Grilling, broiling, charring |
| **Induction** | Electromagnetic (oil) | Very fast, even | Deep frying — oil transfers heat very efficiently |

### Why Oil Fries but Water Boils

Water maxes out at 212°F (100°C) — it can't get hotter at normal pressure.
Oil can reach 350–450°F. This is why:
- Boiled chicken = pale, soft (no Maillard reaction below ~280°F)
- Fried chicken = golden, crisp, complex flavor (Maillard + caramelization
  at 325°F+)
- Steamed vegetables = tender, bright (gentle moist heat)
- Roasted vegetables = caramelized, concentrated (dry heat > 300°F)

### Thermal Mass and Recovery

Heavy pans (cast iron, carbon steel, thick stainless) hold more heat energy.
When cold food hits the pan, a heavy pan's temperature drops less. This is why:
- Cast iron is ideal for searing — it recovers temperature faster
- Thin pans cause steaming (temp drops below boiling point of water on the
  food's surface = steam instead of sear)
- Preheating the pan is non-negotiable for browning

## Enzymatic Reactions

### Enzymes That Matter in Cooking

| Enzyme | Found In | What It Does | How to Use |
|--------|----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Bromelain** | Fresh pineapple | Breaks down protein (tenderizer) | Marinade for tough meat (30 min max or it gets mushy) |
| **Papain** | Papaya | Same as bromelain | Traditional meat tenderizer in tropical cuisines |
| **Amylase** | Saliva, malted barley | Converts starch to sugar | Why chewing bread makes it taste sweet; why malted grains make beer |
| **Polyphenol oxidase** | Apples, avocado, potato | Causes browning when cut (oxidation) | Prevent with acid (lemon juice) or water submersion |
| **Pectinase** | Naturally in fruit | Breaks down pectin (cell walls) | Softens fruit over time; why overripe fruit is mushy |
| **Myrosinase** | Garlic, mustard, horseradish | Produces pungent compounds when cells are crushed | Crush garlic and let sit 10 min — allicin develops (maximum flavor) |
| **Transglutaminase** | Meat glue (commercial) | Cross-links proteins permanently | Binds pieces of meat together; used by chefs for creative cuts |

### The Garlic Rule

Crushing or mincing garlic releases alliinase, which converts alliin to
allicin (the pungent, spicy compound). But heat destroys alliinase. So:
- **Crush, then wait 10 minutes** before cooking: allicin fully develops
  and survives subsequent heat
- **Crush and immediately cook**: minimal allicin, milder garlic flavor
- **Roast whole cloves**: no crushing = no allicin = sweet, mellow, nutty
- This is why recipes that want sharp garlic add it raw at the end (salsa,
  aioli, garlic bread), while recipes wanting mild garlic cook it early

## Water Activity and Preservation

### Why Sugar and Salt Preserve Food

Microorganisms need available water to grow. Sugar and salt both bind water
molecules, making them unavailable to bacteria and mold.

| Preservation Method | How It Works | Examples |
|-------------------|-------------|---------|
| **Salt curing** | Osmosis draws water out; salt binds remaining water | Prosciutto, salt cod, country ham, gravlax |
| **Sugar preserving** | Sugar binds water, creating inhospitable environment | Jam, candied fruit, honey (never spoils), condensed milk |
| **Drying** | Removes water entirely | Beef jerky, dried fruit, sun-dried tomatoes, dried pasta |
| **Smoking** | Drying + antimicrobial phenolic compounds from smoke | Smoked salmon, suho meso, bacon, smoked paprika |
| **Fermentation** | Beneficial microbes produce acid, lowering pH | Kimchi, sauerkraut, yogurt, sourdough |
| **Pickling** | Acid (vinegar) drops pH below where pathogens survive | Pickles, chow-chow, escabeche, achaar |

## Common Cooking Failures: The Science

| Problem | What Happened | The Science | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-------------|-----|
| Steak is gray, not browned | Pan too cold or meat too wet | Maillard reaction needs >280°F; surface water must evaporate first (uses energy, drops pan temp) | Screaming hot pan; pat meat bone-dry; don't crowd |
| Sauce broke (greasy) | Emulsion destabilized | Fat droplets merged back together — too much heat, too fast addition, or not enough emulsifier | Add splash of cold water off heat; whisk vigorously |
| Beans won't soften | Cooked in acidic liquid | Acid strengthens pectin in cell walls, preventing softening | Cook beans in plain salted water FIRST, add acid (tomatoes, vinegar) after they're tender |
| Caramel seized (grainy) | Sugar recrystallized | Undissolved sugar crystals on the pan edge "seeded" the rest | Wash down sides with wet brush; don't stir; add cream of tartar (prevents crystallization) |
| Meat is tough but well-cooked | Wrong method for the cut | Collagen hasn't converted to gelatin (not enough time at temperature) | If already cooked: braise further in liquid at low heat for 1–2 more hours |
| Rice is gummy | Over-stirred, wrong ratio, or lid lifted | Stirring releases surface starch; excess water makes mush; lifting lid releases steam | Don't stir; use correct ratio (1:1.5 for long grain); keep lid on |
| Garlic burned | Added too early at too high heat | Garlic has very little water and high sugar — it browns in seconds | Add garlic after onions are nearly done; cook 30–60 seconds only |
| Green vegetables turned olive | Overcooked in covered pot | Acid from the vegetables (released during cooking) destroys chlorophyll | Cook uncovered, briefly (blanch and shock for vivid green) |
| Fried food is greasy | Oil temperature too low | Cool oil = slow crust formation = oil seeps in before the surface seals | Maintain 325–375°F; use thermometer; don't overcrowd |
